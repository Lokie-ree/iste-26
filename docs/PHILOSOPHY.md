# Discovery-First Pedagogy

Earned reveals and visual confirmation in Grade 8 Geometry.

---

## Understanding Precedes Notation

Traditional mathematics instruction relies on a **notation-first** model: formulas and abstract symbols appear before students understand what they represent. This creates a barrier that prevents many students from reaching "Advanced" achievement levels. When students memorize formulas before grasping underlying logic, they mimic mathematics rather than reason mathematically.

Discovery-first pedagogy inverts this sequence. Students manipulate mathematical objects, observe patterns, and build conceptual understanding before formal notation appears. By designing experiences where **understanding precedes notation**, learning shifts from obligation to discovery.

---

## The Earned Reveal

The **earned reveal** is the intentional delay of formal mathematical language until students have built conceptual grounding through interaction. Symbols appear as labels for ideas students already understand, not as abstract hurdles.

### Three-Phase Design

1. **Manipulation** — Students interact with mathematical properties directly (dragging vertices, predicting transformation outputs, resizing squares on triangle sides)
2. **Pattern Recognition** — Through repeated interaction, students observe consistent behaviors (distances are preserved, areas sum predictably, corresponding distances scale by a constant factor)
3. **Formalization** — Notation appears as confirmation: "What you just discovered—here's the rule"

This sequence lowers the entry barrier for students at "Basic" or "Approaching Basic" levels. High-stakes topics like congruence, similarity, and the Pythagorean Theorem become accessible because understanding is validated through feedback before formal symbols appear.

---

## Visual Confirmation

Traditional assessments measure competence through selection: students choose from pre-defined answers. **Visual confirmation** measures competence through construction: students demonstrate understanding by building or manipulating mathematical objects.

### Comparison

| Visual Confirmation | Traditional Assessment |
|---------------------|------------------------|
| **Active manipulation** — Students interact with mathematical properties | **Passive selection** — Students choose from pre-defined answers |
| **Real-time feedback** — Results are immediate and visually apparent | **Delayed feedback** — Correctness confirmed only after submission |
| **Demonstration of competence** — Mastery proven by construction | **Claims of competence** — Mastery inferred from recognition |

### Example: Rigid Motions Module

Students don't select "reflection over y-axis"—they drag the ghost shape to the predicted position and flip it. When the reveal animation confirms their prediction, they've demonstrated spatial understanding of reflection properties. In the capstone, they go further: sequencing multiple transformations to justify congruence by construction.

### Example: Dilations Module

Students don't pick a scale factor from a list—they resize the ghost shape until it matches the target. When the image snaps into alignment, they've demonstrated that dilation scales distances from a center point by a constant ratio.

### Example: Pythagorean Theorem Module

Students don't recall *a² + b² = c²* from memory—they resize squares built on each side of a right triangle until the two smaller areas visually sum to the larger. The equation appears as a label for a relationship they've already tested.

## Achievement Level Design

Achievement Level Descriptors (ALDs) define proficiency. Discovery-first modules are designed to move students from Level 3 (Basic) to Level 5 (Advanced) by requiring chains of reasoning rather than single-step solutions.

### Level 4 (Mastery) Behaviors

- **Constructing complete responses** — Students justify geometric propositions by testing against interactive models
- **Connecting representations** — Students relate spatial reasoning to coordinate rules (e.g., seeing that reflection over the y-axis maps (x, y) → (−x, y))
- **Describing transformations** — Students articulate what transformations do, not just identify them

### Level 5 (Advanced) Behaviors

- **Describing sequences** — Students build multi-step transformation sequences to justify congruence
- **Composing transformations** — Students discover that rigid motion + dilation produces similarity
- **Proving relationships** — Students use the Pythagorean Theorem to derive the distance formula as a second earned reveal

Visual confirmation provides the evidentiary base for these behaviors. Students test conjectures in real time and critique validity through construction.

---

## Rigor Framework

Rigor is not difficulty—it is the intentional balance of three components. Discovery-first design naturally satisfies all three.

| Rigor Component | Discovery-First Approach | Example |
|-----------------|-------------------------|---------|
| **Conceptual Understanding** | Manipulation before explanation | Students observe that rigid motions preserve distances and angles before congruence is defined |
| **Procedural Skill and Fluency** | Notation as efficient shorthand | Coordinate rules appear after students have mastered spatial transformations |
| **Application** | Modeling and validation cycles | Students formulate predictions, test against visual feedback, and refine understanding |

## Content Cluster Examples

### Rigid Motions (8.G.A.1–3)

Students predict transformation outputs spatially, then see coordinate rules as algebraic descriptions of what they've already mastered. Congruence is earned through the observation that rigid motions preserve distances and angles. The capstone requires students to identify and describe 2-step transformation sequences—justifying congruence through construction.

### Dilations & Similarity (8.G.A.3–4)

Students scale a familiar shape spatially before coordinate rules appear. Similarity is earned by composing a rigid motion with a dilation: students discover that the composition preserves shape but not size, and that the scale factor is the ratio of corresponding side lengths.

### Pythagorean Theorem (8.G.B.6–8)

Students manipulate squares built on each side of a right triangle and observe that the two smaller areas always sum to the larger. The equation *a² + b² = c²* appears as a label for a relationship students have already seen and tested. The distance formula is a second earned reveal: students discover it is the Pythagorean Theorem applied to a coordinate grid, not a new rule to memorize.

## Design Principles Summary

1. **Understanding precedes notation** — Students manipulate, observe patterns, then receive formalization
2. **Earned reveals** — Notation appears as confirmation, not prerequisite
3. **Visual confirmation** — Competence demonstrated through construction, not selection
4. **ALD progression** — Interactions designed to produce evidence of Level 4–5 performance
5. **Rigor balance** — Conceptual understanding, procedural fluency, and application integrated naturally

When these principles hold, the ALD progression becomes an affective one as well: students who enter at Level 3 feeling like geometry is something that happens *to* them leave the capstone feeling like it's something they *did*. When students describe a high-rigor reasoning task as "sick," pedagogy has succeeded: the hardest concepts have become the most engaging to master.
