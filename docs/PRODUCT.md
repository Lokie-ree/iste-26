# Standards Alignment Framework

Mapping discovery-first pedagogy to Louisiana Student Standards for Mathematics (LSSM) — Grade 8 Geometry arc.

---

## Purpose

This document demonstrates how the pedagogical principles in [PHILOSOPHY.md](./philosophy.md) satisfy LSSM rigor requirements and move students across Achievement Level Descriptors (ALDs). It serves as the product specification for the three-module Grade 8 Geometry progression.

---

## Core Pedagogical Principles

Four design principles (defined in [PHILOSOPHY.md](./philosophy.md)) drive all module interactions:

- **Discovery before formula** — Students engage with mathematical phenomena before being handed the formal shorthand.
- **Manipulation before explanation** — Learning begins with tactile or visual movement of mathematical objects, creating a mental model before verbal or written definitions.
- **The "earned reveal"** — Access to formal notation and symbolic representation is granted only after a student has established conceptual grounding.
- **Visual confirmation over multiple choice** — Feedback comes from the success of a visual construction or manipulation, not from selecting a static answer.

These principles map directly to the three components of rigor defined by the Louisiana Department of Education: **Conceptual Understanding**, **Procedural Skill and Fluency**, and **Application**.

---

## LSSM Rigor Components

The LSSM "Guide to Rigor" requires mathematics instruction to balance three components. Discovery-first design naturally satisfies all three:

| Interactive principle   | LSSM alignment & justification |
|-------------------------|--------------------------------|
| **The earned reveal**   | **Conceptual understanding:** Ensures students grasp the "why" of a mathematical relationship before the "how" of its notation, preventing symbolic manipulation without meaning. |
| **Manipulation-first**  | **Procedural skill and fluency:** Builds an intuitive sense of operations before formalizing steps, so fluency is rooted in visual logic. |
| **Visual confirmation** | **Application:** Lets students validate their models against visual outcomes, mirroring real-world cycles of formulation and validation. |

### Example: Visual Confirmation and 8.G.A.2

Standard 8.G.A.2 requires students to *"Describe the effect of dilations, translations, rotations, and reflections on two-dimensional figures using coordinates."* In a discovery-first module, the student's manipulation serves as the primary evidence for their understanding. When a student drags a ghost shape to the predicted position and the reveal animation confirms the match, they have demonstrated understanding through construction—not through selecting a pre-written description.

## Grade 8 Geometry Standards Alignment

The three-module progression covers the full Grade 8 Geometry cluster: congruence through rigid motions, similarity through dilations, and the Pythagorean Theorem with coordinate distance.

| Standard | Discovery-First Interaction | ALD Level Reached |
|----------|----------------------------|-------------------|
| **8.G.A.1** — Properties preserved by rigid motions | Students observe that translations, reflections, and rotations preserve distances and angle measures through repeated prediction tasks | Level 3 (Basic) |
| **8.G.A.2** — Congruence through rigid motions | Students predict image positions for each transformation type, then see coordinate rules as algebraic descriptions of what they've already mastered | Level 4 (Mastery) |
| **8.G.A.3** — Describing transformation sequences | Students build 2-step transformation sequences in the capstone, justifying congruence by construction | Level 5 (Advanced) |
| **8.G.A.4** — Similarity through dilations | Students scale a familiar shape spatially before coordinate rules appear; similarity is earned by composing rigid motion + dilation | Level 5 (Advanced) |
| **8.G.B.6** — Pythagorean Theorem proof | Students manipulate squares on triangle sides and observe that the two smaller areas always sum to the larger | Level 3 (Basic) |
| **8.G.B.7** — Applying the Pythagorean Theorem | Students apply the theorem in multi-step and real-world contexts after the equation is earned | Level 4 (Mastery) |
| **8.G.B.8** — Distance in the coordinate plane | Students discover the distance formula as the Pythagorean Theorem applied to a coordinate grid—a second earned reveal | Level 5 (Advanced) |

## Achievement Level Descriptors (ALDs)

ALDs define student proficiency across five levels. Discovery-first modules are designed to move students from Level 3 (Basic) to Level 5 (Advanced) by requiring demonstration of understanding rather than recognition.

### ALD Progression Through Discovery-First Design

| Level | Performance Characteristics | Module Design Strategy |
|-------|----------------------------|------------------------|
| **Level 3 (Basic)** | Identifying components, recognizing patterns, basic spatial reasoning | Entry point: Students observe and manipulate without formal notation |
| **Level 4 (Mastery)** | Describing transformations with coordinates, determining congruence, applying the theorem | Coordinate reveal: Formalization appears after spatial understanding is demonstrated |
| **Level 5 (Advanced)** | Describing sequences of transformations to justify congruence, deriving distance formula, composing transformations | Capstone tasks: Students build multi-step sequences or derive relationships they've already manipulated |

### Module 1: Rigid Motions ALD Progression

1. **Level 3 Entry** — Students predict transformation outputs spatially (no coordinates visible)
2. **Level 4 Target** — Coordinate rules revealed after mastery; students connect spatial reasoning to algebraic representation
3. **Level 5 Capstone** — Students identify and describe 2-step transformation sequences, justifying congruence

### Module 2: Dilations & Similarity ALD Progression

1. **Level 3 Entry** — Students scale a shape spatially to match a target; no scale factor or coordinate rules visible
2. **Level 4 Target** — Scale factor and center of dilation revealed; students connect spatial scaling to the coordinate rule *(x, y) → (kx, ky)*
3. **Level 5 Capstone** — Students compose a rigid motion with a dilation to produce a similar figure, earning the definition of similarity

### Module 3: Pythagorean Theorem ALD Progression

1. **Level 3 Entry** — Students observe area relationships in squares on triangle sides; find hypotenuse in simple planar cases
2. **Level 4 Target** — Coordinate grid activates; students construct the right triangle between two points and apply the theorem; distance formula appears as earned reveal
3. **Level 5 Capstone** — Students apply the theorem in multi-step problems where the right triangle is not given—constructing it themselves before calculating; 3D extension is a planned stretch goal, not yet validated

This progression is intentional: understanding precedes notation, and notation serves as confirmation rather than prerequisite.

## Module Design Requirements

Every module in the Grade 8 Geometry arc must satisfy these requirements to ensure standards alignment:

### 1. Standards Grounding
- Explicitly map to LSSM content standards (8.G.A.1–3, 8.G.A.4, 8.G.B.6–8)
- Identify target ALD levels (entry, primary target, capstone)
- Document which rigor components are addressed (Conceptual, Procedural, Application)

### 2. Discovery-First Interaction
- Students manipulate before seeing formal notation
- Visual confirmation provides immediate feedback
- Earned reveals appear only after demonstrated understanding

### 3. ALD Progression Evidence
- Design interactions that produce evidence of ALD-level performance
- Level 3: Observation and basic manipulation
- Level 4: Connecting representations (spatial ↔ algebraic)
- Level 5: Multi-step reasoning and justification

### 4. Rigor Balance
- **Conceptual Understanding:** Manipulation reveals "why" before "how"
- **Procedural Fluency:** Notation appears as efficient shorthand for understood concepts
- **Application:** Real-world contexts and modeling cycles where appropriate

### 5. Student Validation
- Modules are tested with actual students in twice-weekly STEM Club sessions before design decisions are finalized
- Iteration is driven by observed behavior (where students stall, what language they use, which reveals land) not by satisfaction surveys
- Design changes are documented against specific observed sessions, not general intuitions

## Summary

Discovery-first pedagogy naturally satisfies LSSM rigor requirements because it requires students to demonstrate understanding through construction rather than recognition. The Grade 8 Geometry arc—Rigid Motions → Dilations & Similarity → Pythagorean Theorem—forms a coherent progression: congruence is built on preserved distances, similarity extends congruence with a scale factor, and the Pythagorean Theorem applies both to coordinate distance. By designing interactions that move students from Level 3 (Basic) to Level 5 (Advanced), modules become primary drivers of achievement rather than supplementary practice tools.
